August 27, 1966

GIPSY MOTH IV

In the summer of 1968, amidst the boundless expanse of the ocean, a daring sailor named Francis Chichester decided to take on the thrilling challenge to circumnavigate the globe in an attempt to conquer the five Great Capes.

With his trusted 16-meter vessel, Gipsy Moth IV, Francis embarked on the solo adventure and unknowingly at the time had launched the beginning of what was to become an international and prestigious competition- The Golden Globe Race.

Chichester's incredible feat inspired countless sailors globally, capturing hearts worldwide. The Sunday Times recounted the gripping tale of a fearless British yachtsman who sailed solo across the world for 226 days, igniting a spirit of exploration and conquest.

June 1, 1968

Sunday Times GGR

A new precedent had now been set, as countless of adventurers prepared to take on the feat of sailing solo nonstop around the world. Under the sponsorship of The Sunday Times, the Golden Globe Race was officially launched in March 1968,

the first ever contest that challenged any willing sailors to circumnavigate the globe in a non-stop solo journey to test the limits of their sailing prowess and human spirit.

With no qualification prerequisites and a prize of £5,000 UK Pounds prize awaiting the speediest voyager, many flocked to take part in attempting to conquer the five great capes and sail into uncharted waters.

The inaugural edition of the Golden Globe race was met with much anticipation and frenzy as the world watched the most unlikely boat- a 9.75 traditional ketch-rigged double-ended yacht named Suhaili- steered by Sir Robin Knox-Johnston emerge as the winner,

with the remaining participants unable to complete the race. Johnston sailed triumphantly into the maritime history books as the first person to complete a solo non-stop circumnavigation of the globe.
